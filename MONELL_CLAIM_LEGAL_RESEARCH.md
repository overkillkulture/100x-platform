## üéØ WHAT IS A MONELL CLAIM?

A **Monell claim** is a civil rights lawsuit under **42 U.S.C. ¬ß 1983** against a **local government or municipality** (city, county, police department, etc.) for violating your constitutional rights.

**Named After:** The landmark Supreme Court case *Monell v. Department of Social Services of the City of New York* (1978)

**What It Does:** Allows you to sue the government entity itself (not just individual officers) when their **official policy or custom** caused your rights to be violated.

---

## üîë KEY LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### Section 1983 - Civil Rights Violations

**42 U.S.C. ¬ß 1983** allows lawsuits against anyone who, "under color of state law," deprives you of constitutional rights.

**Who Can Be Sued:**
- ‚úÖ **Municipalities** (cities, counties, local agencies)
- ‚úÖ **Local government officials** (in official capacity)
- ‚úÖ **Police departments**
- ‚úÖ **School districts**
- ‚ùå **States** (protected by 11th Amendment immunity)
- ‚ùå **Federal government** (different framework)

## üèõÔ∏è THE MONELL CASE (1978)

### Background
- New York City had a **mandatory maternity leave policy**
- Female employees forced to take unpaid leave when pregnant
- Policy violated their constitutional rights
- Employees sued the city itself

### Supreme Court Ruling
**The Court held:**
1. ‚úÖ Local governments are "persons" under Section 1983
2. ‚úÖ Cities/municipalities CAN be sued for civil rights violations
3. ‚ö†Ô∏è BUT only if an **official policy or custom** caused the violation
4. ‚ùå Cannot sue just because an employee did something wrong ("respondeat superior" does not apply)

**Translation:** You can sue the city/county/department, but you must prove their **official policy, custom, or practice** caused your rights to be violated - not just one bad employee acting alone.

---

## üìã REQUIREMENTS TO PROVE MONELL CLAIM

To win a Monell claim, you must prove **3 elements:**

### 1. Constitutional Violation Occurred
- Your rights were actually violated (4th Amendment, 14th Amendment, etc.)
- This happened "under color of state law" (by government actors)

### 2. Municipal Policy or Custom Caused It
You must show ONE of these:

**A. Official Written Policy**
- Example: Department policy says "arrest without warrant for X"
- The written policy itself violates rights

**B. Unofficial Custom or Practice**
- Pattern of behavior so persistent it constitutes policy
- Example: Police department has unwritten practice of racial profiling
- Must show it's widespread, not isolated incidents

**C. Single Decision by Final Policymaker**
- A decision by someone with final authority (mayor, police chief, etc.)
- Example: Sheriff orders deputies to raid all homeless camps
- That single order can establish "policy" for Monell purposes

**D. Inadequate Training or Supervision**
- Municipality's failure to train caused the violation
- The need for training was "obvious"
- Failure to train amounts to "deliberate indifference"

### 3. Causation
- The policy/custom **directly caused** your injury
- Not just present when violation occurred - it must be the cause

---

## üö® THE "CIRCUMVENTING RIGHTS" ISSUE

### Why States Can't Be Sued (But Municipalities Can)

**The Problem:**
- **11th Amendment** gives states sovereign immunity
- You **cannot sue states directly** under Section 1983
- This creates a gap in accountability

**The Workaround - Monell Claims:**
- **Municipalities have NO 11th Amendment protection**
- You CAN sue cities, counties, police departments
- Even though they're technically part of the state

**Example Scenario:**
1. Tennessee state law authorizes shooting fleeing felons
2. Memphis police officer shoots fleeing suspect under this law
3. You want to sue:
   - ‚ùå State of Tennessee - immune (11th Amendment)
   - ‚ö†Ô∏è Individual officer - qualified immunity (likely protected)
   - ‚úÖ **City of Memphis** - Monell claim (can be held liable)

**This is the "circumventing" issue:** States create policies that violate rights, but avoid liability through immunity. Monell allows you to sue the municipality implementing that policy.

## üéØ HOW TO USE MONELL IN YOUR CASE

### Step 1: Identify the Policy or Custom

**Ask yourself:**
- What official policy led to my rights being violated?
- Is there a pattern of behavior (custom)?
- Did someone with final authority make a decision?
- Was there deliberate indifference (failure to train)?

**Examples:**
- Police department policy on use of force
- Sheriff's office practice of ignoring complaints
- City's failure to train officers on constitutional rights
- County jail's custom of denying medical care

### Step 2: Gather Evidence

**You need to show:**
- The policy/custom exists (written documents, witness testimony, pattern evidence)
- It's official or so widespread it's effectively official
- It directly caused your injury
- The municipality knew or should have known it violated rights

**Evidence types:**
- Internal policies and training materials
- Prior complaints with similar facts
- Testimony from other victims
- Expert testimony on standard practices
- Statistical evidence of patterns

### Step 3: File Section 1983 Lawsuit

**Include:**
- Individual defendants (officers, officials) - for qualified immunity issues
- Municipal defendant (city, county, department) - Monell claim
- Clear description of constitutional violation
- Specific policy or custom that caused violation
- Evidence of causation

## ‚ö†Ô∏è COMMON CHALLENGES

### 1. Proving "Policy or Custom"
- **Problem:** One bad cop ‚â† municipal liability
- **Solution:** Show pattern, practice, or official policy

### 2. Qualified Immunity
- **Problem:** Individual officers often protected
- **Solution:** Monell claim targets the entity, not the person

### 3. Deliberate Indifference Standard
- **Problem:** High bar - must show municipality was deliberately indifferent
- **Solution:** Evidence of prior incidents they ignored

### 4. Causation
- **Problem:** Must prove policy caused your specific injury
- **Solution:** Direct connection between policy and harm

## üìö KEY CASES TO KNOW

### Monell v. Dept. of Social Services (1978)
- Original case establishing municipal liability
- Overruled Monroe v. Pape
- Set the "policy or custom" requirement

### Pembaur v. City of Cincinnati (1986)
- Single decision by final policymaker can establish policy
- Don't need long-standing custom if decision-maker has final authority

### City of Canton v. Harris (1989)
- Failure to train can be municipal policy
- Must show "deliberate indifference"
- Need for training must be obvious

### Connick v. Thompson (2011)
- Raised bar for failure-to-train claims
- Pattern of violations often required
- Single incident rarely enough

### Board of County Commissioners v. Brown (1997)
- Single hiring decision usually insufficient
- Need more than one bad hire to show policy

## üõ†Ô∏è PRACTICAL STRATEGY

### When to Use Monell Claims:

**‚úÖ Good for Monell:**
- Repeated incidents showing pattern
- Written policy that violates rights
- Decision by mayor, police chief, or other final authority
- Multiple victims with similar stories
- Evidence municipality ignored prior complaints

**‚ùå Bad for Monell:**
- Single incident by one officer
- Officer clearly violated department policy
- No evidence of prior similar incidents
- Municipality investigated and disciplined officer

### Tactical Considerations:

1. **Sue Both Individual and Municipality**
   - Individual defendants for direct liability
   - Municipality for Monell claim
   - Gives you multiple paths to victory

2. **Discovery is Critical**
   - Get internal policies and training records
   - Prior complaints and disciplinary records
   - Communication between officials

3. **Damages Come from City Budget**
   - Individual qualified immunity doesn't help the city
   - City has deeper pockets than individual officers
   - Better chance of actual recovery

## üéì IMPORTANT LEGAL NOTES

### Statute of Limitations
- Varies by state (usually 1-3 years)
- Starts when you knew or should have known of injury
- Federal claims: Borrowrows state's personal injury statute
- **Act quickly - evidence disappears**

### Exhaustion Requirements
- Usually NO requirement to exhaust administrative remedies
- Can go straight to federal court
- Exception: Prisoner cases (must exhaust under PLRA)

### Attorneys' Fees
- If you win, municipality pays your attorney fees
- 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 1988 allows fee shifting
- Makes it easier to find lawyers for good cases

### Damages Available
- Compensatory damages (actual harm)
- In some cases, punitive damages
- Injunctive relief (stop the policy)
- Declaratory relief (declare policy unconstitutional)
- Attorney's fees and costs
